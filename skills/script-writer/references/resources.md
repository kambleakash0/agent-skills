## 1. The Winston Formula

**Principle:** Quality of communication = f(Knowledge, Practice, Talent), where Talent is the smallest factor.

**Cognitive Basis:** Mastery comes from knowledge and practice, not innate gift. Anyone can become a dramatically better communicator by learning heuristics and applying them deliberately.

**Transcript Example:**
> "It's a matter of how much knowledge you have, how much you practice with that knowledge, and your inherent talent, and notice that the T is very small."
>
> Winston illustrates with the Mary Lou Retton story: an Olympic athlete (maximum T) was a worse skier than Winston because he had the K and the P. "You can get a lot better than people who may have inherent talents if you have the right amount of knowledge."

**Application Across Formats:**

- **All formats:** Treat writing as a skill with learnable rules, not an art dependent on inspiration. Apply specific heuristics deliberately rather than writing "from the gut."

## 2. The Empowerment Promise

**Principle:** Every piece of communication must open by telling the audience exactly what they will know, be able to do, or understand at the end that they do not know now. This is the *reason for being*.

**Cognitive Basis:** People need a reason to invest attention. A promise of empowerment creates a cognitive contract. Jokes fail at the start because the audience hasn't adjusted to the speaker's vocal and cognitive parameters yet.

**Transcript Example:**
> "What you want to do instead is start with empowerment promise. You want to tell people what they're going to know at the end of the hour that they didn't know at the beginning of the hour."
>
> Winston's own example: "At the end of this 60 minutes, you will know things about speaking you don't know now, and something among those things you know will make a difference in your life."

**Hard Rule:** NEVER start with a joke. Jokes at the opening fall flat because "people are still putting their laptops away. They're becoming adjusted to your speaking parameters."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | First spoken paragraph must be the empowerment promise. No throat-clearing, no "Good morning, thanks for having me." |
| **Essay / Article** | The opening paragraph states the transformation: "By the end of this piece, you will understand X." No anecdotal ledes unless they contain the promise. |
| **Email** | The subject line IS the empowerment promise. First sentence of body reinforces it. Example subject: "After reading this, you'll know exactly how to fix the Q3 pipeline." |
| **Social Media Post** | First line is the promise. Hook = promise, not clickbait. "Here's something that will change how you think about X." |
| **Slide Outline** | Slide 1 (after title/collaborators): The empowerment promise in one sentence, spoken aloud, not crammed onto the slide. |

## 3. Cycling

**Principle:** The core thesis must be stated at least three times in meaningfully different ways throughout the text.

**Cognitive Basis:** At any given moment, approximately 20% of the audience is "fogged out." To ensure the probability that everyone absorbs the core idea, you must cycle through it multiple times.

**Transcript Example:**
> "Some people say, tell him what you want to tell him. Tell him again, and then tell him a third time, as if people weren't intelligent. But the point is... at any given moment, about 20% of you will be fogged out no matter what the lecture is. So if you want to ensure that the probability that everybody gets it is high, you need to say it three times."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | State the thesis in the empowerment promise (Cycle 1), restate it in the body using a different angle or example (Cycle 2), and restate it in the contributions/ending (Cycle 3). |
| **Essay / Article** | Thesis in intro paragraph (Cycle 1). Reframe the thesis after the central argument or midpoint (Cycle 2). Restate with full force in the conclusion (Cycle 3). Each cycle should use different words and a different example or framing. |
| **Email** | For emails longer than 3 paragraphs: state the ask/point in sentence 1 (Cycle 1), reinforce in the body (Cycle 2), restate in the closing line (Cycle 3). For short emails: state clearly once, then restate in different words as a closing ask. |
| **Social Media Post** | First line = thesis. Middle = example or reframe. Last line = thesis in punchy form. |
| **Slide Outline** | Dedicate one early slide, one middle slide, and the contributions slide to the same core idea expressed three different ways. |

## 4. Building a Fence

**Principle:** Explicitly state what your idea is NOT, and how it differs from ideas it might be confused with.

**Cognitive Basis:** Ideas exist in a landscape of similar ideas. If you do not explicitly build a fence around yours, the audience will conflate it with something they already know and walk away with the wrong understanding.

**Transcript Example:**
> "If you were from Mars, and I was teaching you about what an arch is, I might say to you, well, that's an arch. And that's not to be confused with some other things... this is not an arch. That's not an arch. I'm building a fence around my idea so that it can be distinguished from somebody else's idea."
>
> Technical example: "My algorithm might seem similar to Jones's algorithm, except his is exponential, and mine's linear."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | After introducing the core idea, add a section: "Now, this might sound like X. It is not X. Here is why..." |
| **Essay / Article** | Dedicate a paragraph or subsection to "What This Is Not." Use direct comparison: "Unlike approach Y, which does Z, this approach does W." |
| **Email** | One sentence of fence-building: "To be clear, I'm not proposing we delay the launch. I'm proposing we change the launch sequence." |
| **Social Media Post** | "This is NOT about [common misconception]. It's about [your actual point]." |
| **Slide Outline** | Include a "What This Is / What This Is Not" comparison slide with minimal text. |

## 5. Verbal Punctuation

**Principle:** Provide explicit transitional landmarks that announce structure, so that anyone who has fogged out can re-board the train of thought.

**Cognitive Basis:** People drift in and out of attention. Without structural signposts, a person who fogs out for 30 seconds is lost for the remainder. Verbal punctuation provides "seams" in the communication where re-entry is possible.

**Transcript Example:**
> "You need to provide some landmark places where you're announcing that it's a good time to get back on."
>
> Winston demonstrates: "The first thing we're going to do is talk about how to start. Then we're going to deal with these four samples, and among these four samples, I've talked about the first idea -- that's cycling. The second idea, building -- and now, the third idea is verbal punctuation. So I'm enumerating and providing numbers. I'm giving you a sense that there's a seam in the talk."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | Use explicit numbered transitions: "That was point one. Now let's move to point two." Never rely on implicit flow. |
| **Essay / Article** | Use subheadings as verbal punctuation. Also use transitional sentences: "Now that we've established X, we turn to Y." |
| **Email** | For multi-point emails, use numbered lists or explicit transitions: "First point... Second... Finally..." |
| **Social Media Post** | Use line breaks and numbering for threads. Each post in a thread should open with a reorienting phrase. |
| **Slide Outline** | Include periodic "roadmap" slides that show where you are in the structure (e.g., highlighting the current section in a list). |

## 6. The Calibrated Question & 7-Second Rule

**Principle:** Ask questions that are carefully calibrated -- not so obvious that answering feels embarrassing, not so hard that no one can respond. Then wait a full 7 seconds.

**Cognitive Basis:** Questions force active cognitive engagement. The 7-second wait creates productive discomfort that activates thinking. Most speakers bail out after 2 seconds, losing the engagement benefit.

**Transcript Example:**
> "Since we're on a roll, can you guess what fourth idea might be here -- an idea that helps people get back on the bus?"
> [Audience answers: "Ask a question."]
> "I will ask a question -- how much dead air can there be? How long can I pause? I counted seven seconds. It seemed like an eternity to me... but that's the standard amount of time you can wait for an answer."
>
> "The question has to be carefully chosen. It can't be too obvious because then people will be embarrassed to say it, but the answers can't be too hard because then nobody will have anything to say."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | Script the question verbatim. Follow it with the stage direction `[PAUSE FOR 7 SECONDS]`. Script a follow-up for if no one answers. |
| **Essay / Article** | Pose a rhetorical question, then leave a paragraph break (the written equivalent of a pause) before answering it. |
| **Email** | End a section with a genuine question. Place it on its own line with white space around it. |
| **Social Media Post** | End the post with a calibrated question. Not "What do you think?" (too vague) but a specific, answerable question. |

## 7. Time and Place

**Principle:** The environment of communication matters. For presentations: 11 AM is optimal, the room must be well-lit, the venue must be cased in advance, and it must be more than half full.

**Cognitive Basis:** Dim rooms signal sleep. Post-meal times create drowsiness. Empty rooms signal irrelevance. Unfamiliar venues create preventable surprises.

**Transcript Example:**
> "11 AM? Yeah. And the reason is most people at MIT are awake by then, and hardly anyone has gone back to sleep."
>
> "We humans, whenever the lights go down... it signals that we should go to sleep."
>
> "Whenever I go somewhere to speak, the first thing I ask my host to do is to take me to the place where I'll be speaking."
>
> On populating the space: "If there were 10 people in this hall, everyone would be wondering, what's going on that's so much more interesting that nobody's here."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | Include a pre-talk checklist in the script notes: lights full up, venue cased, room right-sized. |
| **Essay / Article** | Consider "time and place" as publication context: where will the reader encounter this? Morning newsletter vs. late-night blog post? Adjust tone accordingly. |
| **Email** | Send at optimal reading times. Don't bury critical communications in Friday-evening sends. The "venue" of email is the inbox -- subject line and preview text are your "well-lit room." |
| **Slide Outline** | First note in slide deck preparation: "Lights full up. No dark mode. Slide text must be legible in full lighting." |

## 8. The Board Principle (Informing Mode)

**Principle:** When the purpose is to inform or teach, prefer the equivalent of a blackboard (progressive, handwritten-style revelation) over pre-made slides. Slides are for *exposing* ideas; boards are for *teaching* them.

**Cognitive Basis:** Three properties make boards superior for teaching:

1. **Graphic quality** -- easy to draw and annotate.
2. **Speed property** -- writing speed matches absorption speed. Slides flip too fast.
3. **Target property** -- gives the speaker's hands purpose and the audience a focal point.

**Transcript Example:**
> "I believe that this is the right tool for speaking when your purpose is informing. The slides are good when your purpose is exposing."
>
> "The speed with which you write on the blackboard is approximately the speed at which people can absorb ideas. If you go flipping through a bunch of slides, nobody can go that fast."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | For teaching talks, build in progressive revelation: animate bullet points one at a time, or use a live whiteboard. Never show a completed diagram upfront. |
| **Essay / Article** | Reveal complexity progressively. Don't front-load a complete framework and then explain it. Build the framework piece by piece across paragraphs. |
| **Slide Outline** | Mark slides as [INFORMING] or [EXPOSING]. Informing slides get progressive builds. Exposing slides can be more complete. |

## 9. Empathetic Mirroring

**Principle:** Audiences engage more deeply when they can physically empathize with what the speaker is doing. Physical action and real-world demonstration activate mirror neurons and create embodied understanding.

**Cognitive Basis:** Mirror neurons fire when observing physical actions, making the audience feel as though they are performing the action. This cannot be replicated with static slides or pictures.

**Transcript Example:**
> "When you're sitting up there watching me write on the board, all those little mirror neurons in your head, I believe, become actuated, and you can feel yourself writing on the blackboard. And even more so, when I talk about this steel ball going that way and this way, you can feel the ball as if you were me, and you can't do that with a slide."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | Script physical demonstrations. Include stage directions for gestures, movement, and prop handling. Write what the audience should feel, not just see. |
| **Essay / Article** | Use vivid, kinesthetic language: describe actions, physical sensations, and spatial relationships. "Imagine holding the wheel as it fights your grip" beats "the wheel resists torque." |
| **Email** | Use concrete, action-oriented language. "Walk through the dashboard and click the export button" not "the export feature is available." |

## 10. The Prop Rule

**Principle:** Physical props are disproportionately memorable. They anchor abstract ideas to concrete, tangible objects.

**Cognitive Basis:** Props leverage empathetic mirroring, create narrative tension (like Ibsen's potbellied stove), and become the thing people remember years later.

**Transcript Example:**
> Winston on Ibsen's *Hedda Gabler*: "As the play opens, there's a potbellied stove... as tension mounts in the play, and you see this manuscript, this prop that Ibsen so artfully used, you just know that something's going to happen, because as the play goes on, the fire gets bigger and hotter."
>
> Jim Glass remembering Winston's talk 20 years later: "Oh, yeah, I remember that talk. That's the one where you broke the pointer."
>
> "It's amazing how props tend to be the things that are remembered."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | Script one prop moment. Describe the prop, when it appears, and how it physically demonstrates the idea. Props = tangible anchors for abstract concepts. |
| **Essay / Article** | Use a recurring concrete object or image as a literary "prop." Return to it throughout the piece. Ibsen's stove = your recurring metaphor. |
| **Social Media Post** | Lead with a photo or reference to a concrete object that embodies the abstract idea. |

## 11. Slide Discipline

**Principle:** Slides must be stripped to their absolute minimum. Background removed. Logos removed. Titles removed (say them instead). Minimum font size 40-50pt. No laser pointers (use embedded arrows). Lay out printed slides on a table to check density.

**Cognitive Basis:** The one language processor constraint (see Rule 26). Every word on a slide competes with the speaker for the audience's single language processor.

**Transcript Example:**
> "You have too many [slides], and they have too many words. How did you know, he said... My reply was, because it's always true."
>
> "Step number one is to get rid of the background junk... Step number two is to get rid of the words... we can get rid of the logos... Get rid of the title."
>
> On laser pointers: "You can't use a laser pointer without turning your head... when you do that, you lose contact with the audience."
>
> Font size test: "[Test] what the minimum font size is that's easily legible... 40 or 50. I think that's about right."
>
> Density test: "I ask them to print it out and lay it out on a table. When they do that, it's easy to see whether the talk is too heavy."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Slide Outline** | Every slide annotation must pass: (1) No background art/logos/watermarks. (2) No more than ~10 words. (3) Font 40pt+. (4) No title on slide if speaker will say it. (5) Use embedded arrows, never laser pointer direction. (6) Print-and-table test for density. |
| **Essay / Article** | Analogous: remove filler words, decorative language, and unnecessary headers. Every paragraph should have "air" in it. |
| **Email** | Ruthlessly cut. If your email is longer than a phone screen, it's the email equivalent of a too-heavy slide deck. |
| **Social Media Post** | Maximum density = one idea per post. No thread should attempt to cover what an essay covers. |

## 12. The Hapax Legomenon

**Principle:** You get exactly one moment per piece of communication where you can show something deliberately overwhelming or complex. Use it to make the point that something is incomprehensibly complex. One per presentation, one per paper, one per book.

**Transcript Example:**
> "This is an hapax legomenon. What that means is, this is the kind of slide you can get away with exactly once in your presentation... It's something you in the audience can't understand, and that's the point, but you can't have many of these."
>
> The example: the Afghanistan governance complexity diagram.

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | Script exactly one overwhelming visual. Annotate it: `[HAPAX LEGOMENON: This slide is deliberately complex. The point is the complexity itself.]` |
| **Essay / Article** | One moment of deliberate overwhelming detail (a long list, a dense paragraph) used to make the meta-point about complexity. Flag it to the reader. |

## 13. The Passion Principle

**Principle:** Audiences are inspired when the communicator exhibits genuine passion about the subject.

**Cognitive Basis:** Winston's survey found: freshmen were inspired by teachers who believed in them; senior faculty were inspired by those who showed them problems in new ways; and *everyone* was inspired by someone who exhibited passion.

**Transcript Example:**
> "What I saw from everyone is that they were inspired when someone exhibited passion about what they were doing."
>
> "When I'm lecturing in my AI class, it's natural for me to talk about what I think is cool and how exciting some new idea is."
>
> Resource allocation example: "Isn't that cool? You got to be amazed by stuff that takes a computation from longer than the lifetime of the solar system into a few seconds."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **All formats** | The agent must express why the subject matters to the writer/speaker. Not performative enthusiasm ("Wow! Amazing!") but genuine intellectual excitement about what the idea makes possible. Model Winston's "Isn't that cool?" -- specific, grounded wonder. |

## 14. The Storytelling Animal

**Principle:** Humans think in stories. Teaching people to think means giving them stories, questions to ask about those stories, mechanisms for analyzing them, ways to compose them, and ways to evaluate their reliability.

**Cognitive Basis:** We develop story-processing skills from fairy tales in childhood through professional training. Stories are the fundamental unit of human cognition.

**Transcript Example:**
> "I believe that we are storytelling animals. And that we start developing our story, understanding and manipulating skills with fairy tales in childhood and continue on through professional schools like law, business, medicine, everything."
>
> "You provide them with the stories they need to know, the questions they need to ask about those stories, mechanisms for analyzing those stories, ways of putting stories together, ways of evaluating how reliable a story is."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | Frame the content as a story with characters, tension, and resolution. The audience should feel narrative momentum. |
| **Essay / Article** | Structure the argument as a narrative: setup (the problem), rising action (failed approaches), climax (the insight), resolution (the solution). |
| **Email** | Even short emails benefit from micro-narrative: "We tried X. It broke because of Y. Here's what I propose instead." |

## 15. Situating

**Principle:** Place your work in context. Explain why the problem matters, who else is working on it, and what hasn't worked before.

**Transcript Example:**
> "When you look back on those failures [of oral exams], the most usual reason for people failing an oral exam is failure to situate... it's important to talk about your research in context. This is a problem that's being pursued all over the world. There hasn't been any progress before me in the past 30 years."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | Before presenting your solution, spend 1-2 minutes establishing the landscape: who cares, what's been tried, why it's hard. |
| **Essay / Article** | Include a "Background" or "Context" section early. Not a literature review -- a narrative situating of the problem. |
| **Email** | One sentence of context: "As you know, the team has been struggling with X for three sprints." |

## 16. Practice with Strangers

**Principle:** Never practice a talk only with people who know your work. They will hallucinate content that isn't in your presentation. Practice with people who are unfamiliar with it.

**Transcript Example:**
> "If people know what you're doing, they will hallucinate that there's material in your presentation that isn't there."
>
> "Your faculty supervisor is not a very good person to help you debug a talk because they, in fact, know what you're doing."
>
> "You start the practice session by saying, if you can't make me cry, I won't value [you] as a friend anymore."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **All formats** | When reviewing drafts, have them read by someone unfamiliar with the topic. Ask: "What was unclear? What was missing?" Domain experts will fill gaps unconsciously. |

## 17. The 5-Minute Window (Vision + Done Something)

**Principle:** In a persuasive talk (especially a job talk), you have exactly 5 minutes to establish two things: (1) you have a vision, and (2) you have done something.

**Cognitive Basis:** Evaluators decide early. If the vision and accomplishment aren't established in the first 5 minutes, the rest of the talk is damage control.

**Transcript Example:**
> Winston asks colleagues Delores Etter and Bill Weldon what they look for in faculty candidates:
> "Within one microsecond, Delores said, they have to show us they've got some kind of vision, quickly followed by Bill who said, they have to show us that they've done something."
> "How long does a candidate have to establish these two things? ... Five minutes."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | The first 5 minutes must contain: (1) the problem you see that others don't (vision), and (2) concrete evidence you've made progress (done something). |
| **Essay / Article** | The first two paragraphs must establish vision (what you see) and credibility (what you've done). |
| **Email** | First two sentences: what you want to do (vision) and why you're the one to do it (done something). |

## 18. The Steps Enumeration

**Principle:** To demonstrate that you've "done something," enumerate the steps required to reach the goal, then show which ones you've completed.

**Transcript Example:**
> "How do you express the notion that you've done something? By listing the steps that need to be taken in order to achieve the solution to that problem."
>
> "We need to specify some behavior. We need to enumerate the constraints... We have to implement a system because we're engineers... And we've built such a system, and we're about to demonstrate it to you today."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | After stating the vision, present a numbered list of steps. Indicate which are completed and which are in progress. |
| **Essay / Article** | Use a roadmap paragraph: "Solving this requires three things: A, B, and C. We've achieved A and B. Here's how." |
| **Email** | Bullet the steps and mark status. |

## 19. Winston's Star (The Getting Famous Framework)

**Principle:** For your ideas to be remembered, they need five elements, all starting with S:

1. **Symbol** -- A visual or conceptual icon associated with the work.
2. **Slogan** -- A short phrase that provides a handle on the work.
3. **Surprise** -- Something unexpected that violates the audience's assumptions.
4. **Salient Idea** -- One idea that sticks out above all others (not "important," but "prominent").
5. **Story** -- A narrative of how it was done, how it works, why it matters.

**Cognitive Basis:** Ideas compete for memory. The five S's create multiple retrieval paths, making the idea more likely to be recalled.

**Transcript Example:**
> Winston's own PhD work on arch learning:
>
> - **Symbol:** The arch itself (a visual configuration).
> - **Slogan:** "One shot learning" -- the program learned something definite from every example.
> - **Surprise:** "You don't need a million examples... You can do it with one example."
> - **Salient Idea:** The "near miss" -- examples that almost match but fail in one instructive way.
> - **Story:** The narrative of going from model to near-miss to learning.
>
> "This work on arch learning became mildly famous, and I didn't know why. It was only many years later that I realized that that work accidentally had all of the elements on this star."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | Before drafting, identify all five S's for your idea. If any is missing, develop it. The symbol should appear on slides. The slogan should be stated at least twice. The surprise should land in the middle third. The salient idea should be the one thing the audience repeats to someone else. The story should frame the whole talk. |
| **Essay / Article** | The slogan can become the title or subtitle. The symbol can be a recurring image or metaphor. The surprise belongs in the lede or the turn. The salient idea is the thesis. The story is the structure. |
| **Email** | Subject line = slogan. First sentence = surprise or salient idea. |
| **Social Media Post** | The entire post can be structured as: Symbol (image) + Slogan (first line) + Surprise (the hook) + Story (the body). |

## 20. The Near Miss

**Principle:** When teaching or explaining, use near misses -- examples that are close to the idea but fail in one specific, instructive way.

**Transcript Example:**
> The arch learning example: "This is not an arch, but it doesn't miss by much. So it's a near miss."
>
> Also the general fence-building: "That's not to be confused with some other things that other people might think is -- this is not an arch."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **All formats** | When explaining a concept, provide at least one near miss: "You might think this means X. Close, but no. The difference is Y, and that difference is what matters." |

## 21. The Contributions Ending

**Principle:** The final slide / final paragraph / final section must be labeled "Contributions" and must enumerate what you have specifically contributed. This is NOT a conclusions slide. "Contributions" emphasizes what YOU did. "Conclusions" emphasizes what is generally true.

**Cognitive Basis:** The final slide stays up during questions and as people file out. It is the last impression. Wasting it on "Questions?" or "Thank you" squanders the most valuable real estate in the entire presentation.

**Transcript Example:**
> "If you say these are my conclusions, these are perfectly legitimate conclusions that nobody cares about. What they care about is what you have done. And that's why your final slide should have this label, contributions."
>
> "Your final slide, the one that's up there while people are asking questions and filing out, it ought to be the one that has your contributions on it."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | Final slide title: "Contributions." List 3-5 specific things you contributed. This slide stays up during Q&A. |
| **Essay / Article** | Final section: "What This Contributes" or equivalent. Not "In conclusion" -- state what has been added to the reader's understanding. |
| **Email** | Final line restates the specific value or action: "This gives us X, Y, and Z that we didn't have before." |

## 22. The Anti-Thank-You Rule

**Principle:** Never end with "Thank you." It is a weak move that implies the audience endured your talk out of politeness.

**Cognitive Basis:** "When you say thank you, even worse, thank you for listening, it suggests that everybody has stayed that long out of politeness and that they had a profound desire to be somewhere else."

**Transcript Example:**
> "I don't recommend it. It's a weak move."
>
> Winston shows Bill Clinton deliberately avoiding "thank you": "At one point, he seems to be almost pressing his lips together, forcing himself not to say thank you."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | Do NOT script "Thank you" as the final words. Use a salute, a joke, or a benediction instead. |
| **Essay / Article** | Do not end with "Thanks for reading." End with the contributions or a salute. |
| **Email** | "Thanks" as a sign-off is culturally expected in email and is acceptable as a **closing salutation**, not as the final substantive content. The last substantive sentence should be a contribution or call to action. |

---

## 23. The Salute Ending

**Principle:** End by saluting the audience -- expressing genuine appreciation for their context, their work, or the time you've spent in their environment.

**Transcript Example:**
> "You can salute the audience. And by that, I mean, you can say something about how much you value your time at a place."
>
> Winston demonstrates: "It's been great fun being here. It's been fascinating to see what you folks are doing here at MIT. I've been much stimulated and provoked by the kinds of questions you've been asking."
>
> His actual ending: "I'm glad you're here. And the reason is by being here, I think you have demonstrated an understanding that how you present and how you package your ideas is an important thing. And I salute you for that."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | Script a genuine salute: acknowledge the audience's expertise, their commitment to the topic, or the quality of their engagement. |
| **Essay / Article** | Close by acknowledging the reader's investment: "If you've made it this far, you understand why this matters -- and that understanding is itself the first step." |

---

## 24. The Joke Placement Rule

**Principle:** Jokes fail at the beginning (audience isn't adjusted) but work at the end (audience is attuned and ready).

**Transcript Example:**
> Beginning: "I don't recommend it... in the beginning of a talk, people are still putting their laptops away. They're becoming adjusted to your speaking parameters... They usually fall flat."
>
> End: Doug Lenat's advice: "I always finish with a joke, and that way, people think they've had fun the whole time."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | Never script a joke in the first 2 minutes. A joke in the final 2 minutes is effective. |
| **Essay / Article** | A witty or surprising closing line works. A "funny" opening line usually doesn't. |

---

## 25. The Collaborator Placement Rule

**Principle:** Acknowledge collaborators on the first slide, not the last. A last-slide collaborator list diminishes your individual contribution.

**Transcript Example:**
> "When you show a whole gigantic list of collaborators at the end of a talk, it's a kind of let down because it suggests that nobody knows... did you do anything significant?"
>
> "Where do you do that? Not on the last slide, on the first slide."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | Slide 1 or 2: list collaborators. Final slide: contributions only. |
| **Essay / Article** | Acknowledgments in the first section or footnote, not the closing paragraph. |

---

## 26. The One Language Processor Constraint

**Principle:** Humans have a single language processor. If it is engaged in reading, it cannot simultaneously listen. If it is engaged in browsing, it cannot absorb the speaker.

**Cognitive Basis:** This is the master constraint that governs all slide design, all decisions about text density, and all choices about competing information channels.

**Transcript Example:**
> "We humans only have one language processor. And if your language processor is engaged browsing the web or reading your email, you're distracted."
>
> "We have only one language processor, and we can either use it to read stuff or to listen to the speaker."
>
> Student experiment: "What did the subjects remember best, what he said, or what they read on the slide? And the answer is, what they read on the slide... one of the subjects said, I wish you hadn't talked so much. It was distracting."

**Application Across Formats:**

| Format | How to Apply |
| --- | --- |
| **Presentation Script** | Never have the audience reading dense text while you are speaking. If a text slide is necessary, pause and let them read it before speaking. |
| **Slide Outline** | Slides are condiments, not the main course. Words on slides must be minimal so the audience listens to the speaker. |
| **Essay / Article** | Don't force the reader to process two competing information streams (e.g., dense footnotes competing with body text, or a sidebar that requires reading in parallel). |

## Quick-Reference: Rules by Format

### Presentation Scripts

1. Open with empowerment promise, never a joke
2. Cycle the thesis 3 times
3. Build a fence around the core idea
4. Use verbal punctuation (numbered transitions)
5. Script calibrated questions with `[PAUSE FOR 7 SECONDS]`
6. Check time and place (lights up, room cased, right-sized)
7. Use board/progressive revelation for teaching; slides for exposing
8. Script physical demonstrations and props
9. Slides: no logos, no background, no titles, 40pt+ font, embedded arrows
10. Allow one hapax legomenon
11. Express genuine passion
12. Situate the work in context
13. Establish vision + done something in first 5 minutes
14. Enumerate steps
15. Apply Winston's Star (Symbol, Slogan, Surprise, Salient, Story)
16. Final slide = Contributions
17. Collaborators on first slide
18. End with salute, joke, or benediction -- never "Thank you"

### Essays / Articles

1. Opening paragraph = empowerment promise
2. Cycle thesis: intro, midpoint, conclusion
3. Dedicate a paragraph to "What This Is Not"
4. Subheadings and transitional sentences as verbal punctuation
5. Pose rhetorical questions with paragraph-break pauses
6. Build ideas progressively (board principle)
7. Use kinesthetic, vivid language (empathetic mirroring)
8. Use a recurring concrete image as a literary prop
9. Keep paragraphs airy -- no walls of text (slide discipline analog)
10. One moment of deliberate complexity (hapax legomenon)
11. Express genuine intellectual excitement
12. Frame content as a narrative (storytelling animal)
13. Situate the argument early
14. First two paragraphs: vision + credibility
15. Roadmap paragraph for steps
16. Apply Winston's Star
17. Final section = Contributions, not "In conclusion"
18. Never end with "Thanks for reading"

### Emails

1. Subject line = empowerment promise
2. First sentence reinforces the promise
3. For 3+ paragraph emails: cycle the point 3 times
4. One sentence of fence-building
5. Numbered lists for multi-point emails
6. One sentence of context (situating)
7. Concrete, action-oriented language
8. Ruthlessly short -- one phone screen max
9. Final substantive sentence = contribution or call to action
10. "Thanks" as sign-off only, never as final content

### Social Media Posts

1. First line = empowerment promise (hook = promise, not clickbait)
2. First line thesis, middle example, last line thesis (cycling)
3. "This is NOT about X. It's about Y." (fence)
4. Line breaks and numbering for threads (verbal punctuation)
5. End with a calibrated question
6. Lead with a concrete image (prop)
7. One idea per post (slide discipline)
8. Apply slogan + surprise from Winston's Star

### Slide Outlines

1. Slide 1: title + collaborators
2. Slide 2: empowerment promise (spoken, not crammed)
3. Periodic roadmap slides (verbal punctuation)
4. All slides: no logos, no background, no clutter, 40pt+ font
5. Progressive builds for teaching slides
6. Embedded arrows instead of laser pointer directions
7. One hapax legomenon slide maximum
8. Print-and-table density test
9. Final slide: "Contributions" with 3-5 bullet points
10. Slides are condiments to the speaker, not the main event
